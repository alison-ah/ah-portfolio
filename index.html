When websites don’t work for everyone, they frustrate and exclude.

Tiny text, low-contrast colors, missing headings, or hard-to-use buttons can make it difficult—or impossible—for some people to use a site.

Accessibility builds trust and better experiences for all.

Accessible design helps people with disabilities, and it also makes things easier for everyone: people using search engines, translating content, or reading in bright sunlight.

Accessible websites are easy to use so more people can and will keep coming back.

I create websites and digital experiences that almost anyone can use and I teach others how to do the same.

FAQs

How would I know if my website is accessible?

Use free resources like AIM’s suite of WAVE® evaluation tools to check for the most common digital accessibility issues.

What are the most common issues and how do I fix them?

According to WebAIM, “96% of all WCAG 2 errors detected fall into six categories: low contrast text, missing alternative text for images, missing form input labels, empty links, empty buttons, and missing document language. Addressing these issues would significantly improve accessibility across the web.”

I also created a Accessibility Guide for New Developers after spotting many similar issues in developer portfolios.

Do you build new websites, or just fix accessibility issues on existing ones?

I do both. I offer full-site builds with accessibility baked in from the start, or I can jump into an existing project to improve accessibility without starting over. Whether you’re launching something new or making what you have better, I can help.

What’s your approach to web development beyond accessibility?

Clean, maintainable code. User-first design. Tools and content that are easy to keep up-to-date. I build with long-term usability in mind—not just for end users, but for the people managing the site behind the scenes.
